Masa# 'alal Khuffayn

Khuffayn are a special type of socks upon which one can do masa# (wipe wet hands) rather than wash the feet in wuḍū'.

Conditions for the Khuff In order to do masa# upon a khuff, the following conditions must be met:

1.The khuff must be worn after washing the feet in wuḍū' before wuḍū' is broken again.

2.The khuff must cover the feet up to (and including) the ankles.

3.If the socks are not leather, they should be strong enough that a person can walk wearing them for three miles without being torn apart.

4.Each khuff must be free from any tear that is of a size equal to three of the smallest toe.

5.The khuff must be waterproof. Water must not be able to seep through.

6.The khuff should remain in position (covering the foreleg) without being tied.

7.The material of the khuff should not be transparent (see through).

Different Types of Khuff It is permissible to perform masa# over full leather socks and those socks which are not leather but have all the conditions needed for masa# to be correct. It is permissible to do masa# over mujalladayn (leather on top and bottom of the socks). It is also permissible to do masa# over muna'alayn (leather only on the bottom and thick on top). With regards to non-leather socks that do not have any of the qualities of the modern range of waterproof socks, they must meet the requirements of thickness in order for masa# to be valid. It is impermissible to perform masa# on non-leather thin socks like the cotton and nylon socks we commonly wear.

For example: Zayd performs wuḍū' at 6pm thereafter puts on the khuffayn. He then breaks his wuḍū' at 7pm. His time shall begin at 7pm when he broke his wuḍū'. Therefore the maximum time he can keep his khuffayn on is until 7pm the next day if he is a muqīm.

Masa# on Wounds

Even when a person is wounded, he still has to perform ṣalāh. Islām has given us ways to perform wuḍū' and ghusl despite being injured. If a wound can be washed without any harm by removing the bandage or plaster, then a person must wash the wound and perform wuḍū' or ghusl as normal. If the wound cannot be washed but can be wiped without any harm then one should wipe the wound.

However, if a wound cannot be washed or wiped thoroughly due to the problem increasing or it is not possible to remove the cast, bandage or plaster, then one should do masa# over the wound, cast, bandage or plaster and complete wuḍū' normally.

Feeding Others

Rasūlullāh said:

"The most virtuous charity is feeding a hungry creature."

(Bayhaq|)

Charity in Arabic is known as ṣadaqah. In another ḥadīth Rasūlullāh said, "Hasten towards giving ṣadaqah for calamities cannot come to a person who gives ṣadaqah." Here Rasūlullāh is telling us that to feed the hungry is the best type of ṣadaqah a person can do. The word 'creature' includes all those that are alive, which means that even if you feed a hungry animal you will get this reward. Feeding anyone is a great deed in itself, but when someone is in need of food, at such a time it will be more rewarding. We should always try our best to help those who are in need, even if it may be little. We don't know which of our actions is liked by Allāh the most.

Sīrah so far... When the persecutions and tortures increased, our Beloved Messenger granted permission for some Companions to migrate to Abyssinia. The ﬁrst migration consisted of twelve men and four women. Thereafter, a second migration took place, consisting of eighty-three men and eighteen women.

Opponents of Islām amongst the Quraysh sent a delegation to the King of Abyssinia with the intention of putting fear and hatred of Muslims in the hearts of Abyssinians. Against their will, the King of Abyssinia respected the Muslims after hearing the message of Islām from Ja'far and the enemies returned unsuccessful.

Following this, they tried a different approach and boycotted the tribes of Ban[ Hāshim and Ban[ Muṭṭalib for three years. Six months after the end of this boycott, the wife of our Beloved Messenger Muḥammad , Khadījah , and his uncle, Abū Ṭālib both passed away.

The Messenger went to Ṭā'if to invite the tribes to Islām. In their ignorant desire to be rid of the Prophet , the townsfolk stoned him.

Allāh took Rasūlullāh on a special journey. The two parts of that miraculous journey are known as Isrā' and Mi'rāj, where he was taken to 'the farthest masjid', Masjid al-Aq\, and then up into the Heavens to see many great things and meet other messengers. In the eleventh year of Prophethood the message of Islām began to spread in the city of Yathrib, about 450 kilometres to the north of Makkah.

The First Pledge Of 'Aqabah In the year following Al-Isrā' and Al-Mi'rāj, the twelfth year of Prophethood, a group of twelve people came from Yathrib (Madīnah). They pledged (promised) to believe in one Allāh and that Muḥammad is His Messenger. They promised not to steal, kill, or to do any other acts of evil. This pledge was taken near a hillock (small hill). A hillock in Arabic is called 'aqabah, therefore it is known as the Pledge of 'Aqabah. After this promise, the Messenger sent Mu'ab ibn 'Umayr to Madīnah Munawwarah as the ﬁrst Muslim teacher.

Mu'ab fulﬁlled his duties preparing the ground for the arrival of the Messenger .

Every house in Madīnah Munawwarah echoed with the call of Taw#|d (belief in the Oneness of Allāh).

The Second Pledge Of 'Aqabah The next year during the pilgrimage season, Mu'ab ibn 'Umayr returned to Makkah along with seventy new Muslims. Shortly after arriving, they made contact with the Beloved Messenger and agreed to meet in secret. They met at night near the hillock where the ﬁrst meeting had taken place. There the people of Madīnah Munawwarah promised to protect the Messenger from that which they would protect their women and children. They also promised him ﬁve other things:

1.To listen and obey in difﬁculty and ease.

2.To spend in the way of Allāh at all times.

3.To tell others to do good and stop them from evil.

4.Not to fear the harsh words of anyone whilst in the service of Allāh .

5.To aid the Messenger when he made the journey to Madīnah Munawwarah. The Messenger then asked the group to choose twelve people to represent their people in matters regarding the pledge. The people of Madīnah Munawwarah kept to their word and never broke their promise. We must also try and be like the people of Madīnah Munawwarah.

The Plot After the second pledge of 'Aqabah, the Messenger announced that the Muslims of Makkah Mukarramah were to migrate (move) to Madīnah Munawwarah. The Muslims left everything in Makkah and slowly, one at a time, or in small groups, began to migrate to Madīnah. When the Quraysh realised that all those who had accepted Islām as their way of life were moving to Madīnah, they became very angry. They decided the only way to put an end to this was to kill the Messenger . He was to be killed by a group of young men: a man would be selected from each of the main tribes so that the Ban[ Hāshim would not be able to seek revenge against all the tribes of Makkah. While they plotted, Abū Bakr hired two camels and a guide for the journey. The Makkans knew the Messenger was going to leave Makkah soon. The young men assigned to murder the Messenger surrounded the house during the darkest hour and settled to wait for dawn when they would carry out their evil plan.

Hijrah

Trust

"Surely, Allāh commands you to deliver trusts to those entitled to them, and that, when you judge between people, judge with justice. Surely, excellent is the advice Allāh gives you. Surely, Allāh is All-Hearing, All-Seeing." (Qur'ān 4:58) Our Beloved Messenger said: "Three qualities single out a hypocrite, even if he prays or fasts and claims to be Muslim: If he speaks, he lies. If he makes a promise, he does not keep it. If he is entrusted, he betrays the trust." (Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī) Our Beloved Messenger Muḥammad said, "There is no faith for the one who has no trust."

(Aḥmad)

Trust is a very important aspect in our lives. If there is no trust between people, they will begin to feel insecure and unsafe. Our Beloved Messenger Muḥammad was so trustworthy that he was given the title Al-Amīn (The Trustworthy). He never betrayed the trust of the one who entrusted him.

When Rasūlullāh was migrating to Madīnah Munawwarāh, he left all the trusts with 'Al| so he could return them back to the rightful owners. An amazing story is mentioned in Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī on the authority of Ibn 'Umar as follows:

One day three men were out on a journey when a change in the weather forced them to enter a cave. As they entered, a large boulder fell and blocked the entrance. They each decided to ask Allāh through one special action which had been done only for the pleasure of Allāh .

As the ﬁrst one prayed to Allāh , a third of the boulder moved; the second man's prayer caused another third to move. The third man then prayed to Allāh , "O Allāh! I hired some labourers and paid them their wages, except one of them who departed without taking his right. I kept this trust of his and invested his money, purchasing cattle. The money generated a lot of proﬁts. After a long time, he came to me and said, "O slave of Allāh! Pay me my right." I said, "All that you see is yours - camels, cattle, goats and slaves." He replied, "O slave of Allāh! Do not joke with me." I assured him I was serious. So he took everything and left nothing. The man then said, "O Allāh! If I did so seeking Your pleasure, then please relieve us of our distress." The rock slipped aside and they all left the cave, walking freely.

Allāh informs us of the qualities of the people who will enter the highest level of Jannah: 'Al Firdaws'.

… 'And (success is attained) by those who honestly look after their trusts and covenant… Those are the inheritors, who will inherit al-Firdaws (the Paradise). They will be there forever.' (Qur'ān 23:8,10-11)

One of the qualities is for those people to keep their trusts and return them to their rightful owners.

There are many categories of trust: a person gives us an item to look after, so they have entrusted us and it is our duty to look after it, returning it once they request it. Another example is when someone comes to us to ask our advice and trusts us to give them the best advice. In a ḥadīth, our Beloved Messenger said, "The one who is consulted is in a position of trust." (Tirmidhī) This means that we should not tell them something which is incorrect or may cause harm. In this situation we are even permitted to do ghībah. For example, Aḥmad asks Muḥammad how a certain person is because he wishes to do business with him. Now Aḥmad has entrusted Muḥammad to tell him the truth. If he has knowledge that this person is a thief or disloyal, it is necessary for him to tell Aḥmad. Muḥammad will not be sinning if he speaks bad about this person because he is trying to save Aḥmad from harm.

Our Beloved Messenger Muḥammad said, "Gatherings are held in trust..."

(Abū Dāwūd) When you are sitting with people whilst they are discussing something which they don't want others to know, they have now entrusted you. If you then go and inform other people about what was discussed, you have broken their trust. This aspect of trust is often forgotten and we very quickly spread other people's secrets without realising that we commit a sin as we tell others. This usually results in corruption and hatred amongst the ummah. May Allāh protect us, Āmīn. We can now understand the broadness of the word amānah (trust), as it covers many things from physical items to actual words.

Seeking Permission

Before Entering "O you who believe, do not enter any houses, other than your own houses unless you seek permission and greet the residents with salām. That is good for you, so that you may be heedful. Even if you do not ﬁnd anyone in them, do not enter therein until permission is given to you. And if it is said to you, 'Go back', just go back; it is more decent for you. Allāh is All-Aware of what you do." (Qur'ān 24:27-28)

Kildah ibn Al-Hanbal reports that he visited the Prophet and entered his house without seeking permission. So he said, "Go back and say:

'Assalāmu 'alaykum. May I come in?'"

(Abū Dāwūd)

Before Entering Taking permission before entering any private places such as houses, an individual room, or even a vehicle, displays high levels of manners. It protects a person from being startled and shocked by a newcomer whom they were not expecting. It protects those from seeing or hearing things which were not meant to be seen or heard. At times, people in their private areas are more relaxed in their dress than when in public. If we just walk in without asking permission, it may cause great embarrassment.

Sometimes people are in their own thoughts and going about their chores. Think for a moment: imagine yourself working at home, thinking you are alone. You are too busy concentrating that you don't hear the door opening. Suddenly you look up and see someone standing in your room! Can you think of the shock?

Before Entering Islām has laid down the best manners. We have been given guidance by our Beloved Messenger on how to lead our lives in the best manner possible in order to avoid all unnecessary headache. The main reason for asking permission is to avoid seeing those things which the person inside does not wish you to see: those things which he wants to keep private. So if we are waiting outside a door for permission or maybe we are passing a house where a door has been left open, we should on no account look or peep inside.

Allāh informs us in the Qur'ān that even children in their home should ask permission at certain times, those times when a person is most likely to be in relaxed clothing.

Before Entering Look at the beauty of Islām - it has taken into consideration all angles of a person's feelings and privacy. A person should feel safe in his own space; he should know that no one will intrude into his personal space without permission. This is giving everybody their own personal rights!

If, per chance, we hear some noise from the house of people talking, we must not stop and try to eavesdrop. For our Beloved Messenger Muḥammad has said, "… and he who seeks to listen to the talk of people (secretly) will have molten lead poured into his ears on the Day of Resurrection." (Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī)

Before Entering It does not concern us what they are talking about, so why should we listen? Many a time we get involved in those things that have nothing to do with us. Remember Allāh will not ask us about the matters of other people's personal lives, but He will question us about our own personal lives and time. So rather than worrying about things such as, "Let me see where my neighbour is going. What is he speaking about to that person? How and where did he get the money to buy that? Why is he standing there? He must be up to something..." We must see that such questions have no beneﬁt and could lead a person towards sin. Our Beloved Messenger instructed us to stay away from such things.

Before Entering Abū Hurayrah reports that the Messenger said, "From the beauty of a person's Islām is to leave those things which do not concern him." (Tirmidhī) If a nation or a community could manage to develop such great manners, the manners Islām has given us, what a peaceful life everyone in that society would have. But change begins with us! We cannot expect to change the world if we ourselves do not decide to change. If every individual thought that their actions or changes in behaviour Akhlāq meant nothing, then none of us would ever reach any goals at all!

Before Entering One day a king ordered the inhabitants of a certain village to ﬁll a well with milk. Each person was to pour one cup of milk at night. One old lady came to the well thinking that if she were to add just one glass of water, then what difference would it make? What could be the harm? In the morning the whole well was full of water!

Removing Harm

from the Road "And whatever good you will send ahead for your own-selves, you will ﬁnd it with Allāh much better in condition, and much greater in reward."

(Qur'ān 73:20)

Abū Hurayrah reports that the Messenger of Allāh said, "Removing harm from the road is ṣadaqah." (Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī)

Removing something harmful has been called ṣadaqah. Ṣadaqah is known as charity in Islām and our Beloved Messenger Muḥammad said, "Give ṣadaqah without delay, for it stands in the way of calamity." (Tirmidhī) It is such a simple act, so easily done, yet we are still rewarded for it. There is a story mentioned in Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī about a person who was walking one day, and saw a branch which was obstructing the pathway. He decided to move it out of the way to make it easy for people to pass. Allāh loved this action so much that he forgave him and granted him Jannāh because of this one deed.

Sub#ānallāh how merciful and kind is our Lord!

from the Road Rasūlullāh said that "Īmān has sixty or seventy branches: The least of them is to remove harm from the road…"

(Muslim, Abū Dāwūd)

Since it is a part of īmān, it shows how important this act is. Let us think for a moment: why is there so much importance placed on such a small action? When looking over many a#ādīth, one reason that can be understood is that Allāh loves a person helping another and detests a person who hurts another. When you remove something harmful from the road, you did not directly help a person, but you may have saved a person from getting hurt and falling into difﬁculty.

from the Road What makes this action more special is that you do not know who you are helping or how many people will beneﬁt from your small efforts.

Imagine you are walking and you see a banana peel in the middle of the pathway: you pick it up and place it in the bin. After carrying out this ṣadaqah, you go away.

Shortly after, a child comes running down the road, followed by an elder. Then comes a person who cannot see so clearly, and thereafter a person in a wheelchair. Think if the banana peel had still been there. The child could have broken his leg, and the elder may have slipped and hurt himself. Similar things could have happened to the visually impaired person or the wheelchair user. All these people were saved through one action of yours! So in fact you helped them more than you can imagine.

from the Road A small deed it may seem, but the results of carrying it out or ignoring it can be very different.

Abū Barzah al-Aslam| said, 'I said, "O Messenger of Allāh , show me an action by which I will enter Jannah!" He said, 'Remove harmful things from people's path."'

(Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim)

from the Road Sadly, today we see a very different picture.

Many people, instead of removing harm from the road and pathways, create harm for people. We sometimes forget that others will walk after us on the road we are strolling along. If someone falls or gets hurt because of our carelessness, what will happen to us when the sigh of this person comes out - will it not be against us? Has our Beloved Messenger not warned us to be aware of the du'ā' of the oppressed one?

"Beware of the supplication of the oppressed one as there is no veil between it and Allāh ." (Tirmidhī) Is it really worth it? Are we so lazy that we can't wait till we ﬁnd a dustbin? Should we be eating whilst we are walking anyway? What if it was us who slipped and fractured our bone? These are questions we need to ask ourselves before we throw rubbish.

from the Road The Prophet Muḥammad said: "Charity is prescribed for each descendant of Ādam every day the sun rises." He was then asked, "From what do we give charity every day?" The Prophet answered, "The doors of goodness are many...enjoining good, forbidding evil, removing harm from the road, … all of these are charity prescribed for you." (Bayhaq|)

Neighbour What we need to understand is that the way of life that was brought down to mankind through our Beloved Messenger from Allāh is not restricted and limited to just acts of worship alone. Neighbour This is the starting point and the end point: we must not be a means of harm for anybody. The closest people to us after our immediate families are our neighbours.
