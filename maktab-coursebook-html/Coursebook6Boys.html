Water

When a person wants to pray to Allāh by engaging in ṣalāh, ṭawāf, sajdah tilāwah or even for the purpose of holding the Qur'ān, they must be pure and in the state of wuḍū'. When a person wants to recite the Qur'ān without touching it they can do so without wuḍū', however they must not be in the state of major impurity. The way to become pure is to perform wuḍū' or ghusl. The following can be used to become pure for ṣalāh:

Rainwater Ocean/seawater River water Well water All types of ﬂowing water e.g. water in pipes, which have no sign of impurity.

Large water and small water There are different types of water:

Large water is that amount of water which covers more than 21 square metres which is equivalent to 4.57m x 4.57m and is deep enough that a person can take out water without touching the ground.

Small water is that which covers a lesser area than the above amount.

Small amounts of water will become impure by just a drop of impurity falling into them. So if a drop of blood falls into a small amount of water, the entire water becomes impure.

Large water in which some najāsah has fallen will become impure if the colour, taste or smell changes.

Used water A person cannot make wuḍū' or ghusl with used water.

Pure water is regarded as 'used' the moment it separates from the body. For example, a person who is washing their arm will make the water 'used' the moment it separates from the arm.

Impurities after drying are of two types:

Invisible impurities Dried invisible impurities cannot be seen after drying. For example urine, semen, dog saliva, pig sweat etc. To make the impure area pure, wash the affected area three times and wring the cloth tightly after each wash.

Delaying a Rukn Sajdah as-Sahw becomes compulsory when a person delays from moving from one rukn (posture) to another. For example, if a person carries on sitting for a couple of seconds after reciting Tashuhhud in the 2nd rak'ah in a four rak'ah ṣalāh, Sajdah as-Sahw becomes compulsory.

Doubts in ṣalāh If one is unsure of the number of rak'āt they have offered before ﬁnishing the ṣalāh, and this is the ﬁrst time that the doubt has arisen, or they rarely get doubts, then they should start the ṣalāh again. If a person has a doubt after the ṣalāh about the number of rak'āt they have offered or feel they might have missed a wājib, they should not pay attention to the doubt unless certain that they have made a mistake. If a person keeps developing doubts every so often whilst in ṣalāh regarding the number of rak'āt offered, then they should act upon what they think is most likely. For example, if a person is unsure whether the rak'ah they are offering is the third or fourth, but they most likely think it is the third rak'ah, they should assume it as the third rak'ah. Thereafter, they should perform Sajdah as-Sahw. If a person is unsure of which rak'ah they are in, then they will assume it to be the lesser of the two and sit in both rak'āt. For example a person is unsure whether they are in the third or fourth rak'ah, they will assume it to be the third and sit for qa'dah in the third and in the fourth rak'ah. Thereafter they will perform Sajdah as-Sahw.

What if a third person joins the two? If there is no space to move back and there is only space to move forward, the imām should move two steps forward upon the arrival of the third person. The imām should not prolong the prayer so much that it genuinely tires or troubles the congregation.

Every new row should begin from the middle and thereafter the congregation should continue joining to the right and the left.

Janāzah

The life of this world is temporary. We will all return to Allāh sooner or later. The biggest challenge we face is leaving this world with Īmān. If a Muslim passes away with Īmān, they will be granted Paradise forever. If a person dies without Īmān, they will be doomed to eternal punishment.

What to do when a person is passing away When a person is in the last stages of life, make them face the Qiblah if possible. Recite the Kalimah aloud so that the dying person can hear. Don't order or force them to read. Sit calmly and keep reciting the Kalimah next to them. The family members should sit calmly reciting Sūrah Yāsīn next to the dying person. The moment the person passes away, close their eyes and close the jaw if left open.

Make haste in preparing the deceased for burial.

Ghusl of the deceased Do not comb or cut the hair of the deceased or clip their nails but remove all jewellery, any dentures etc.

Cover the private area with a cloth.

Remove all clothing including underwear by cutting.

Block nostrils, ears and mouth with cotton wool to prevent water entering during ghusl.

Wearing gloves but without viewing, massage the stomach gently and wash both private parts. Wash any najāsah which comes out.

Perform wuḍū' on the deceased without pouring water into the deceased's nostrils or mouth.

The appropriate sequence is to wash: a.The face b.The arms to the elbows c.The head, simply by performing masa# d.The feet up to the ankles If the deceased passed away in the state of major impurity, then the mouth and nose need to be wetted by making use of pieces of wet cotton wool.

Wearing thick gloves, wash the private area with a cloth. After wuḍū', the head and beard should be washed.

Tilt the body onto its left side and, starting from the head, pour water and scrub with soap all along the body ensuring that water reaches the left-hand underside.

Thereafter, tilt the body onto its right-hand side and wash in a similar manner.

Now raise the top half of the body (into almost sitting position) and gently massage the stomach. If any waste emerges, wipe and wash it away. There is no need to repeat the wuḍū' or ghusl.

Repeat this whole process twice more.

Next, dry the body with a clean towel ensuring the private area of the deceased is covered at all times.

Jumu'ah Ṣalāh

The best day of the week is Jumu'ah. Allāh has gifted us a special ṣalāh on this day.

Jumu'ah ṣalāh is compulsory upon men who are muqīm (residents) and are able.

Jumu'ah is different from other ṣalāh. It has a khuṭbah with it in the Arabic language before the ṣalāh and it has two adhāns. For the ṣalāh of Jumu'ah to be valid, there must be a khuṭbah before it.

There must be a minimum of four men altogether (imām and three muqtadīs) for Jumu'ah ṣalāh. One will become the imām and the rest will be the jamā'ah. The moment the ﬁrst adhān is called, Muslims must leave all business and prepare to go to the masjid. When the imām appears for the khuṭbah, we must observe silence, listen attentively and not do any of the following:

1)Perform ṣalāh 2)Read books 3)Eat 4)Talk 5)Reply to the salām of others 6)Play around with mobile phones

Adhān and Iqāmah

How the adhān began Ibn 'Umar narrated that when the Muslims arrived at Madīnah, they used to assemble for the prayer, and used to guess the time for it.

During those days, the practice of adhān for the prayers had not been introduced yet.

Once they discussed this problem regarding the call for prayer. Some people suggested the use of a bell like the Christians, others proposed a trumpet like the horn used by the Jews, but 'Umar was the ﬁrst to suggest that a man should call (the people) for the prayer; so Allāh's Messenger ordered Bilāl to get up and call for prayers.

In another narration ‘Abdullāh ibn Zayd reported: "When the Messenger ordered a bell to be made so that it might be struck to gather the people for prayer, a man carrying a bell in his hand appeared to me while I was asleep, and I said; servant of Allāh, will you sell the bell? He asked; what will you do with it? I replied; "We shall use it to call the people to prayer." He said; "Should I not suggest you something better than that." I replied: "Certainly." Then he taught me the words of adhān and iqāmah. When the morning came, I came to the Messenger and informed him of what I had seen in the dream. He said: It is a genuine vision, and he then said, teach it to Bilāl, for he has a louder voice than you have. So I got up along with Bilāl and began to teach it to him and he used it in making the call to prayer. 'Umar ibn al-kha{{āb heard it while he was in his house and came out trailing his cloak and said: O Messenger of Allāh. By him who has sent you with the truth, I too have seen what he saw. The Messenger said: To Allāh be the praise. (Abū Dāwūd)

Adhān is the call to prayer. Allāh has blessed us with a beautiful way to call the Muslims to ṣalāh. It is a sunnah to call the adhān for the ﬁve daily far} prayers. The Mu'adhin (caller of adhān) should face the Qiblah with wuḍū' when calling the adhān. He should call the adhān calmly. The following actions are disliked during adhān:

1)To sing the adhān 2)To call the adhān without wuḍū'

3)For a child to call the adhān 4)For a woman to call the adhān 5)To sit while calling the adhān 6)For the caller of adhān to talk in between.

Iqāmah is the call to make people stand up and prepare the rows for ṣalāh. The Mu'adhin who called the adhān should say the iqāmah as well. The iqāmah should be said more quickly than the adhān.

Our Beloved Prophet Muḥammad is the only person in history who has been described in Sīrah so much detail. Allāh has preserved the Prophet in every detail. His appearance The Prophet was neither tall nor was he short (he was of medium stature). However, when he walked with people, Allāh would make it seem as though the Prophet was the tallest. Nobody else would tower over him. In complexion, he was neither very white like lime, nor very dark, nor so very brown that he reﬂected darkness. He had a wheat-coloured complexion with redness in his cheeks. His face had a natural glow to it.

The hair of the Prophet was neither very straight nor very curly (but slightly wavy). He had jet black hair which reached his blessed earlobes and at times was long enough to reach his shoulders. His hair would be parted in the middle. His blessed beard was full and dense. He did not have a big body. His face was not fully round nor was it fully elongated, but was moderately between the two. The Prophet had large eyes. The pupils of his eyes were extremely black. He had a few red lines in the whiteness of his eyes. His extremely black eyes with his fair glowing face increased his beauty.

His body was of even proportion. His chest and stomach did not protrude, while his chest was broad and wide. The space between his shoulders was wide. The bones of his joints were strong and large (denoting strength). The Prophet 's skin was naturally very soft.

Anas narrated: "I served Allāh's Messenger for ten years. He never said, 'humph' to me. Abū Bakr

A-Ṣiddīq Early life Abū Bakr was from the Ban[ Taym clan.

Holding an esteemed position amongst the Quraysh's nobility, he was assigned to matters that related to debts and blood money. He was an expert on genealogy and possessed great knowledge on the history of the Quraysh and other Arab tribes.

'Ā'ishah relates that the Messenger of Allāh said, 'Verily Abū Bakr is the most knowledgeable person among the Quraysh about the lineages of the Quraysh.' (Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim)

Abū Bakr was also one of the most respected and honest businessmen of Makkah. He would trade with people with fairness and the people would trust him with their business. He was very generous even before the advent of Islām.

A-Ṣiddīq We ﬁnd that love and respect are earned through certain qualities a person may possess. Keeping this in mind we ﬁnd it easy to understand that Abū SīrahBakr was universally loved by the Quraysh: the noblemen loved him because he displayed such great character which beﬁtted noblemen; the poor loved him for his generosity; the scholars respected him for his in-depth knowledge and the businessmen revered him for his fairness and honesty.

Despite the fact that alcohol was permissible to drink till the verses announcing its impermissibility were revealed in Madīnah Munawwarah, Abū Bakr (just like our Beloved Messenger Muḥammad ) never drank alcohol in his entire life.

A-Ṣiddīq Accepting Islām For many years Abū Bakr had been searching for the truth. He was one of the few who never prostrated in front of any idol before accepting Islām so he already knew that the false gods of the pagan Arabs were not from the truth. When the Beloved Messenger presented Islām to Abū Bakr he readily accepted the message and pledged allegiance to the Messenger of Allāh without any hesitation. The Beloved Messenger once said to his companions that whenever he invited a person to Islām, that person would hesitate before making up his mind to embrace Islām. This was not the case with Abū Bakr , who neither hesitated nor wavered, but instead immediately embraced Islām and believed in the Messenger of Allāh . His accepting Islām gave a meaningful purpose to his existence: he was able to channel all the feelings of goodness he had into Islām.

Indeed, Abū Bakr 's acceptance of Islām was a great moral support for our Beloved Messenger Muḥammad .

A-Ṣiddīq Spreading the Message of Islām Abū Bakr realised that Islām was not a religion to keep to himself only.

Immediately after accepting Islām, Abū Bakr began inviting others to this beautiful way of life. He did not limit his happiness in ﬁnding the truth to himself but drove himself to share this happiness with others. On the very ﬁrst day of his accepting Islām, Abū Bakr invited his fellow friends and associates. After seeing and hearing the message, his efforts began to bear fruit and the following men all accepted Islām:

A-Ṣiddīq Trials and Tribulations When the disbelievers of Makkah saw the Muslims preaching to other Makkans the true message of Islām, they felt threatened in regards to their leadership and from that day on, everyone who accepted Islām was a common enemy, even if he was a nobleman like Abū Bakr . Moderation in Expenditure Everything we have is a blessing of Allāh . The clothes we wear, the food we eat, the house we live in, the belongings we have and everything else. It is all from Allāh . Allāh out of His mercy and kindness blessed us with these things. If Allāh wants, He can take away these blessings from us without even asking us.

Allāh warns us about His blessings:

“If you express gratitude, I shall certainly give you more, and if you are ungrateful, then My punishment is severe.” (Qur'ān 14:7)

Therefore, if we want these blessings to remain and increase, we must follow what Allāh wants us to do with these blessings. Allāh wants us to be grateful to Him. One of the ways to be grateful is by being moderate and not wasting blessings. Allāh reminds us in many different places in the Qur'ān not to be wasteful.

Allāh describes his special and close servants in Sūrah al-Furqān. One of the qualities He mentions of his close servants is that they do not waste money and they spend with moderation.

Allāh says:

"(My special servants) are those who when they spend, do not spend wastefully nor do they spend less, rather they are moderate in between."
