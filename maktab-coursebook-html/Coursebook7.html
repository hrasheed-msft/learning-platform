Sutrah

We should never walk directly across a person performing ṣalāh, i.e. between that person and the direction of Qiblah. The Prophet said:

"If the one crossing in front of a praying person knew what sin is upon him, it would be better for him to wait for forty than walk across." Abū An-Naḍr (one of the narrators of the ḥadīth) said: "I don't know whether he said forty days, months, or years." (Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī) The Prophet would place a spear or an item in front of himself when performing ṣalāh in an open space. He would do this so people could pass by in front of him without passing directly across him whilst he was busy in ṣalāh. Using or placing such an item for this purpose is called 'sutrah'. A sutrah can be anything like a table, chair, stick, pole etc placed on one side with the condition that it is:

  • an arm's length.
  • at least as thick as a ﬁnger.
  • upright.

The sutrah of an imām is sufﬁcient for the congregation.

Qaṣr Ṣalāh

If a person has an intention of travelling more than 54 miles (87 km), they will be regarded as a traveller (mus\ﬁr) in Islām. A traveller can shorten their ṣalāh, performing it as what is called 'qaṣr'. Qaṣr is to perform two rak'āt far} for Ḍhuhr, 'Aṣr and 'Ishā' instead of four. A person will be eligible to perform qaṣr the moment they leave the boundaries of their home town up until they reach back to the boundaries of their home town.

Furthermore if a person intends to stay at a place for less than 15 days, they too will perform qaṣr at the destination.

However if a person intends to stay at a destination for 15 days or more, then they will perform the full prayer and not qaṣr. Note: they will become a mus\ﬁr once again when they leave to travel towards home or towards another destination more than 54 miles. If a person doesn't know how long they will be staying at a destination, they will continuously perform qaṣr even if they end up staying for 15 days or more. Ṣalātul Marīḍ

If a person cannot stand up for ṣalāh due to an injury, he should sit on the ﬂoor and perform ṣalāh. If a person cannot perform sajdah, he may also sit on the ﬂoor and perform ṣalāh with gestures. He will incline his head towards the ground for rukū' and sajdah. The inclination of the head for sajdah should be lower than that of rukū'. If a person cannot sit down and pray ṣalāh, it is permissible to lie down on his back or side and perform ṣalāh by gestures and indication.

If, due to extreme illness or unconsciousness, a person cannot even nod his head for six prayers or more, he will not have to make them up.

Ṣalātul Ma'dhūr

At times, people suffer from illnesses whereby they cannot keep their wuḍū' at all. For example, a bleeding wound which doesn't stop bleeding at all during an entire ṣalāh time. If a person experiences the problem from the entry time of any far} ṣalāh until the expiry time of the ṣalāh in such a way that they are unable to ﬁnd enough time required to make wuḍū' and perform the far} ṣalāh without the problem occurring, they will then be regarded as a ma'dhūr.

Once a person is regarded as a ma'dhūr, the illness must continue at least once in every ṣalāh time for this condition to remain in place.

Once a person is regarded as a ma'dhūr, the occurrence of the illness will not break the wuḍū'. They may carry out any actions of worship in that ṣalāh time. Wuḍū' will break with the ending of the ṣalāh time.

However, if any other nawāqi} occur then the wuḍū' will break. For example a ma'dhūr person suffering from constant bleeding goes to the toilet and relieves himself or bleeds from another location, his wuḍū' will break.

Zakāh

Zakāh is a monetary act of worship that has been ordained in the shar|'ah. It is one of the ﬁve pillars of Islām.

Zakāh literally means 'to increase' and it also means 'to purify'. Both of these meanings are found through the action of zakāh. Allāh puriﬁes us and our wealth and furthermore grants barakah in our wealth and increases our reward. By not paying zakāh, the punishment is severe. One āyah should sufﬁce as a warning here:

"Those who hoard gold and silver and do not spend in the way of Allāh, announce to them a most grievous penalty. On the day it (the wealth) will be heated up in the ﬁre of Jahannam, then their foreheads and their sides and their backs shall be branded with it: 'This is what you had accumulated for yourselves. So, taste what you have been accumulating.'" (Qur'ān 9:34-35)

For zakāh to be compulsory, you must:

1)Be a Muslim 2)Be mature 3)Own the amount which makes zakāh wājib. The amount is called 'niāb'.

4)Own the 'niāb' amount after the passing of one lunar year.

Niāb The niāb is the amount of money which you must have before zakāh becomes compulsory.

Niāb is calculated after adding up all the zakātable items that you have in hand. Thereafter, deduct any debts you owe in the space of one year.

Niāb is equivalent to the value of 612.36 grams of silver or 87.48 grams of gold. However, the niāb is always calculated according to the lowest of the two prices (gold or silver).

The day you ﬁrst own the niāb amount after maturity, (at this time) pencil this Islamic date in your diary. It makes no difference whether the wealth increases or decreases during the course of the year unless it reaches zero.

One lunar year later, if you possess the current niāb amount on that very same Islamic date after deducting % debts, you must pay 2.5 as zakāh.

Inheritance

What happens to a person’s wealth after they die?

Islām is a complete way of life. Our Beloved Messenger Muḥammad has informed us in regards to matters of life, death and what happens after death. When a person dies their wealth will be used and distributed in the following manner:

1.Burial and shrouding - The expense to pay for burial and shrouding will be taken from the deceased person’s wealth (unless another party takes this responsibility). If any money remains after this then move onto stage 2.

2.All debts - The deceased person’s wealth will be used to pay off any outstanding debts. If there is no money to pay the debts, the family should try their best to settle this.

3.The Bequeath (Will) – The deceased person’s bequeath will now be given. Examples of a bequeath is where a person wrote down a will before he died that he wants such and such an amount to be given to an orphanage or masjid etc.

The bequeath should meet the following conditions:

  • Only 1/3 of the remaining wealth can be bequeathed.
  • The bequeath should not be for any of those family

members who will be receiving a share after this stage.

  • The bequeath should not be for any impermissible

cause.

4.After these three steps, whatever money is left will be distributed to the family according to the shares stipulated in Islām.

I'tikāf

I'tikāf means to seclude oneself and stay in the Masjid.

'Ā'ishah narrates that the Beloved Messen- ger Muḥammad would perform I'tikāf during the last ten days of Ramaḍān until Allāh took him away, then his wives performed I'tikāf after him. (Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī)

Abū Hurayrah narrates that the Qur'an would be recited to the Beloved Messenger once every year. It was recited to him twice the year he passed away. He would perform I'tikāf for ten days every year and he performed I'tikāf for twenty days the year he passed away. (Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī) It is Sunnah Mu'akkadah (emphasised) to perform I'tikāf in the last ten days of Ramaḍān.

However, this is a collective sunnah, so, if one person stays in I'tikāf in the local masjid, the rest of the people who live near the masjid will not be sinful. If nobody performs I'tikāf in the masjid, all those who were able to perform I'tikāf who live around the masjid will be sinful.

A person in I'tikāf must stay within the actual masjid whilst in I'tikāf. He is not allowed to leave the masjid without a valid excuse.

There are three types of valid excuses and reasons a person can leave the masjid:

  • A valid shar'| reason – for example, if Jumu'ah Ṣalāh is

not performed there or if a person needs to perform far} ghusl, he will be permitted to exit the masjid boundary to perform it.

  • A natural reason – for example, a person needing to use

the toilet will be permitted to leave the masjid.

  • A necessity – for example, there is a ﬁre in the masjid.

For such a reason, a person will be allowed to evacuate the masjid. However, he should immediately go to a nearby masjid and continue with his I'tikāf. If a person exits the masjid for any other reason, their Sunnah Mu'akkadah I'tikāf will be broken and they will have to make up (qaḍā') for that day.

What can a person in I'tikāf do: A person in I'tikāf will do the following in the masjid:

  • Eating
  • Sleeping
  • Drinking.

The following are disliked in the masjid:

  • Talking about worldly

things

  • To sell things in the

Masjid.

Besides Ramaḍān, a person may also perform Naﬂ (optional) I'tikāf. This is simply making intention of I'tikaf when entering a Masjid. A person should make an intention of I'tikaf whenever he enters a Masjid.

Ḥalāl Foods

Allāh says in the Noble Qur'ān:

"O Believers! Eat of the good and pure (lawful) that We have provided you with and be grateful to Allāh , if you truly worship Him."

(Qur'ān 2:172) The Prophet said, "A body nourished by ḥarām will not enter Paradise."(Ṭabarān|) The above shows how important it is to eat ḥalāl.

Ḥarām will have an impact on your soul, remove blessings from your life and leave your prayers unanswered.

Food purchased using money that is earned unlawfully will also result in the food being ḥarām.

Land animals and slaughtering Land animals which are ḥalāl (such as cattle, sheep, goats and camels) must be slaughtered in a speciﬁc way before they can be consumed.

Likewise, chicken and other ḥalāl birds must be slaughtered before they can be consumed. It is necessary to sever at least three of the four vessels in the neck:

1)Trachea (windpipe)

2)Oesophagus (food pipe)

3)Two jugular veins The above must be cut by a Muslim whilst saying Bismillāhi Allāhu Akbar. This is called 'Tasmiyah'.

Once a Muslim cuts the vessels of a live animal whilst reciting the Tasmiyah, the animal will be ḥalāl.

There are many conventional methods to slaughter animals. If any method fails to meet any of the above requirements, the animals slaughtered by any such method will not be ḥalāl to consume.

Islām has given animals many rights. The following highlights the value and rights that animals have: It is severely disliked to cause any undue pain to the animal. A Muslim should not slaughter an animal in front other animals. This will scare the other animals. To keep cutting the throat more than needed or to cut off the entire head is severely disliked.

Animals must not be cruelly transported, handled or dragged to the place of slaughter.

Cross-contamination Another point to remember with food is the possibility of contamination. In many restaurants, takeaways and food outlets, there is a mixture of ḥalāl and ḥarām foods served. A Muslim should abstain from eating from any place which serves ḥalāl and ḥarām food.

Many a time the ḥarām foods and ḥalāl foods are cooked in the same oil. Other times, the workers touch the ḥarām products and thereafter touch ḥalāl products without washing their hands. All such actions render the ḥalāl products unlawful to consume even if they do not contain ḥarām ingredients.

Uḍḥiyah

Zayd ibn Arqam reports that the Companions of Rasūlullāh asked him: "Ya Rasūlallāh, what is this sacriﬁce?" He said: "It is the way of your forefather Ibrāhīm ." They asked: "What (reward) is for us therein?" He replied: "There is a reward for every hair." They asked: "For the wool, Ya Rasūlallāh ?" He replied: "There is one reward for every strand of wool." (Aḥmad)

Uḍḥiyah refers to the animal sacriﬁce offered in the days of Ḥajj. This is also commonly called 'qurbān|'.

Qurbān| is wājib (compulsory) upon every mature Muslim male and female who owns assets in excess of their personal needs equal to or more than the niāb amount between 10-12th of Dhul Ḥijjah, and they are not a mus\ﬁr (traveller). The time of slaughtering depends where the slaughtering is taking place. If the slaughtering is taking place in a city, then the slaughtering of qurbān| can only begin after 'Īd Ṣalāh. If the slaughtering takes place in a village, provided there is no 'Īd Ṣalāh, slaughtering can begin after the break of dawn (Sub# a-]ādiq).

Conditions for the qurbān| animal The animal selected for qurbān| should be healthy and free from defects. The animals which can be sacriﬁced are the following:

1)Sheep 2)Goats 3)Cattle 4)Camels The animals can be male or female. They must be the following age:

Goats and sheep have to be at least 1 year old.

Sheep which look like they are 1 year old may be used provided they are at least 6 months old.

Cattle must be at least 2 years old.

Camels must be at least 5 years old.

Sheep and goats can only be on behalf of one individual. Cattle and camels have seven shares. A group of seven people can purchase one cow and have it slaughtered on their collective behalf. It is preferable to distribute the meat of qurbān| into the following 3 portions:

1)1/3 for oneself 2)1/3 for the neighbours, family and friends 3)1/3 for the poor. If one desires to keep all the meat for oneself, he may do so. It is desirable for a person who is performing qurbān| that they do not clip their nails or remove hair from the beginning of Dhul Ḥijjah until the qurbān| is done.

Spreading Rumours

"O you who believe, if a sinful person brings you a report, verify its correctness, lest you should harm a people out of ignorance, and then become remorseful on what you did." (Qur'ān 49:6)

Anas ibn Mālik narrates that the Messenger Muḥammad said: "News of something is not like seeing it." (Ṭabarāni)

What is meant by a false rumour? A rumour is some kind of news or information, generally about one or more people, that is spread without thought for its authenticity or accuracy. Starting or spreading a rumour is to fabricate news that is not true, and that may lead to misunderstanding between brothers, sisters or spouses.

Many a time we receive dubious information from a person about something or someone and we are keen to spread it without checking the truth of the matter.

Allāh informs us in the above verse that whenever news reaches us from someone who is not so trustworthy we must ﬁrst of all substantiate it, check and verify if it is really true.

Frequently, a person takes action on news they have just heard and then later realises that the news was not true. The end result is usually regret and sorrow.

How many friendships have been broken because of false rumours? How many families are left broken due to the evil of false words ﬂoating around? This is a major cause of disunity!

Let us make a promise that we will never react to what someone randomly informs us of, and instead we shall respond only after having thoroughly investigated the matter.

Communication is the key! A person may be jealous and wish to break your friendship. They will be very successful if you take what they say at face value. They only need to tell you that your best friend said such- and-such a small and measly thing about you, and it will set doubt or malice in your heart.

If someone comes to us and tells us that such-and- such a person has done something unpleasant, then ﬁrst of all we should declare their words to be ghībah, an awful sin. If we can't help but hear these words of slanderous ghībah, if they somehow sink in, then the least that we should do is avoid spreading them. We do not even need to investigate the matter, as it has nothing to do with us. Our Beloved Messenger Muḥammad said:

"From the beauty of a person's faith is that he leaves aside that which does not concern him."

Why should we be so nosey and try to ﬁnd out about other people's issues? Have we no other things to do than gossip? Isn't Allāh going to question us on the Day of Judgement with regard to this? He most certainly is!

Mu'āwiyah wrote to Mughīrah , asking him to write back with a ḥadīth from our Beloved Messenger Muḥammad , so Mughīrah wrote: "I heard our Beloved Messenger Muḥammad say, 'Allāh has disliked for you to indulge in idle talk, to ask too many questions, and to waste your wealth.'" (Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī)

Let us not waste the precious time that Allāh has blessed us with in talking about useless things. By all means, if the matter relates to us personally then we should investigate it by asking the person. In this way we can be absolutely sure who is speaking the truth and who has been lying to us.

Remember never to make a swift reaction! Instead, you should respond with wisdom (#ikmah). The difference between a knee-jerk reaction and a considered response is that with the former you are not using your judgement but your emotions!

Instead of this you should take time to reﬂect, to check the facts, and not to act on impulse.

Make this a rule in your life and you will see how peaceful your life can become, in sh\ Allāh.

The act of spreading incorrect news is becoming much more commonplace nowadays. We might receive an email or a message and forward it without thinking too much about it being true or false. Just because it is a text message or email, doesn't mean that it isn't a rumour! We must check the facts before forwarding it!

Remember: just because you hear or read it, doesn't mean you should repeat it.

Ḥafs ibn 'Āṣim narrated that the Messenger Muḥammad said, "It is enough for a person to be judged as a liar that he spreads all that he hears." (Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim)

(In other words, once you violate your oaths to hide your mischief, it may prompt others to be mischievous and fraudulent too. This is what 'slipping' means. In such a case you will be responsible for their deviation from the right path). The Beloved Messenger Muḥammad said:

"(Of the) major sins are: to ascribe partners to Allāh, disobey parents, murder someone, and to take a false oath (intentionally)" (Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī)

Answering a Question If your friend or someone in the class has been asked a question you shouldn't rush to answer it until you are offered the chance.

Luqmān the wise once said to his son: 'If another person was asked a question never hasten to give the answer, as if a great prize is at stake. By doing so, you will belittle the questioner, you will offend the questioned and you will join the ranks of those who are detested by others due to ill manners.

Using a mobile phone When calling someone, keep in mind the following points: Be conscious of the time of day. If you are calling abroad, work out what time it is in that particular country. If you are calling a scholar or an elder, then keep in mind that they might be working to their timetable. It may be their rest time, so disturbing them would be disrespectful. If there is no answer, do not keep calling the same number again and again. A scholar once mentioned that he received over 100 missed calls from the same number whilst he was resting! If the person is not picking up it means one of three things: they are busy, or they do not wish to talk to you at that moment in time, or their phone is not accessible to them. Instead you should leave a message so that they can get back to you if they want. Do not be intrusive. When you call, ask if it is an appropriate time to speak. Inform the person of the purpose of your call and how long you intend to speak to them.

E.g. 'Assalāmu 'alaykum I hope you are OK and well. I just needed ten minutes of your time to discuss a few issues. Are you free to talk now or can I call at a more convenient time?' This will make the person more attentive to you and will allow your matter to be sorted out in a better way.

Ensure your phone is on silent when going to the masjid. Imagine because of your phone ringing in ṣalāh someone loses his concentration. Have a simple ring tone and do not keep musical tones. It is very disrespectful to sit with your phone texting away whilst someone is sitting with you engaged in conversation. Put your phone away when you are sitting with other people especially when you are with your family. Unfortunately this has become a bad habit in our society that many people are sitting in a room yet no one is talking to each other, instead each person is busy with their own phone. How pitiful is our state!

If you need to make or take a call when you are a guest or a host, seek permission with the people sitting around you.

E.g.'Sorry I hope you don't mind but I just need to take/make this call. I won't be long.'

"O you who believe, fear Allah, and speak in straightforward words. (If you do so,) Allah will correct your deeds for your beneﬁt, and forgive your sins for you. Whoever obeys Allah and His Messenger achieves a great success." (Qur'ān 33:70-71)

'Ā'ishāh narrates that the Beloved Messenger Muḥammad 's talk was so brief that you could count his words'.

(Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī)

Speak in a clear tone. Do not raise your voice. Do not backbite. Do not say things which you may regret later.

Speak to elders with respect. Do not interrupt the other person.

Always remember to introduce yourself clearly.

Using the Internet The Internet is indeed a great tool which we can beneﬁt from greatly yet at the same time if we don't take care then it can be very dangerous. A few points to bear in mind whilst using the Internet: Do not go on the Internet without a reason. Don't just go to surf for the sake of surﬁng for it is then that many people get caught in the web and end up wasting valuable hours.

Learn your dīn through your teachers and scholars: not any random site. If you are using the Internet to gain some knowledge check that the site you are visiting to access these masā'il is reliable.

There are many people on the Internet who are surﬁng to prey on innocent people, therefore do not give away your personal details to any stranger.

Remember everything is not what it seems on the 'net': don't be fooled by others. Be vigilant. Do not click on 'pop-ups' for they will lead you to other sites and, before you realise, you will be very far from where you began. Before forwarding any article or news, verify its authenticity.

Walking with elders and seniors The proper manner of walking with elders was laid out by Imām Ya'l\ al-Ḥanbal| . He told his student on one occasion: 'if you walk with someone you honour you should walk to his right. Place him at the position of the imām in the prayer (when there is only one muqtad| he will stand on the right hand side of the imam), leave his left clear in case he needs to spit or to get rid of dirt.'

To begin with the elders when serving It is Sunnah to begin with those who are the eldest or high in merit such as a scholar and thereafter to continue to serve from his right. The commentators of ḥadīth have noted that the Sunnah is to continue serving with those on the right of the most notable person even if that person happened to be less important than the person on the left. If all present are of equal merit and age then start from the right. The person serving should partake of the food or drink last.

Informing dependants of your whereabouts Islām has taken into consideration everyone's feelings and emotions. When you leave your home you should inform your family where you are going and also inform them of your time of return in advance. This will keep their mind at ease and they will know how to reach you should they need to contact you.

Interaction with non-Muslims Islām is a way of life which teaches us to be kind and caring towards all of Allāh 's Creation. If your neighbours or peers are non-Muslims, be helpful to them, come across with good manners.

Displaying good manners will also allow them to see the beauty of Islām and remove any negative image they may have regarding Muslims in general. If they are ill, visit them and if a relative of theirs has died, offer your condolences. Imām Abū Ḥanīfah was asked how to console a Jewish person or a Christian who had lost a child or relative he said: "Say: 'Allāh has decreed death for all his Creation. We ask Allāh to make death the best fate to wait for. We all belong to Allāh and to him we all shall return. Be patient and endure this calamity.'"

Anas ibn Mālik said that a Jewish boy used to serve the Prophet preparing his ablution and bringing him his shoes. The boy became ill, and the Prophet went to visit him. He found him gravely ill with his father sitting by his side. The Beloved Prophet invited the boy to Islām by asking him to say: 'There is no God but Allāh.' The boy looked at his father who kept silent. The Prophet repeated his request and the boy again looked at his father who told him: "Obey Abul Qāsim (Rasūlullāh was also known as Abul Qāsim)." Just before dying the boy said: "I bear witness that there is no God but Allāh and that you are His Messenger". The Prophet said, I thank Allāh for enabling me to save him. (Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī)

Expressing condolences If you need to break some tragic news to another person, do so sensitively. Do not start by saying:

"Have you heard who has had an accident?" or "Do you know who just died." Prepare the person to bear the news. Choose the right moment. If you receive news of a person who has lost a loved one, console them in the best way. Mention how one day everyone will have to part from this world.

Explain to them that their loved one is surely in a better place now. Remind them of the rewards of patience. Offering condolences is an important action at a brother or sister's moment of sorrow, if done with sincerity.

"A person who consoles other Muslims suffering from a calamity will be awarded a dress of dignity by Allāh on the Day of Judgement." (Ibn Mājah)

Pray for the deceased when offering condolences. The Messenger said to Umm Salamah at the death of her husband, "O Allāh, forgive Abū Salamah, elevate his status among the guided people, and look after the family that he left behind. O our Lord of the Universe, forgive us and him, comfort him in his grave, and lighten his stay."

(Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim) The Prophet said, "The rights of a Muslim towards another Muslim are ﬁve: to return a greeting (salām); to visit them when they are ill; to follow their funerals; to accept their invitations; and to respond the one who sneezes."
